3rd Development Goal
Health and Wellbeing

Are you familiar with the third Sustainable Development Goal? - Let’s start from the beginning.

It's a global effort to reduce neonatal and child mortality rates by ensuring access to affordable interventions, immunizations, and quality healthcare. In this blog post, we'll explore this important topic in more detail, discussing why children are dying from preventable causes and what can be done to reduce mortality rates. Join me as we delve into this critical issue and discover how we can all play a role in creating a healthier and more sustainable world for generations to come.

The third Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) aims to improve global health and wellbeing by reducing neonatal mortality to at least as low as 12 per 1,000 live births (1.2%) and under-five mortality to at least as low as 25 per 1,000 live births (2.5%) by 2030.

Before we delve into the strategies aimed at reducing mortality rates, let's first explore the root causes of why children are dying unnecessarily.


Most children and newborns are dying from preventable causes such as preterm birth complications (Breathing problems · Feeding difficulties · Cerebral palsy · Developmental delay · Vision problems · Hearing problems), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) or also called birth trauma which are physical as well as psychological impairments of the baby or the mother that occur as a result of the birth process, pneumonia (Lungenentzündung), diarrhoea, and malaria. These causes can all be treated or prevented with access to affordable interventions, immunization, adequate nutrition, safe water and food, and quality care by trained health providers. It is essential to address these preventable causes to reduce child mortality rates.

Older children from 5 to 9 have seen one of the largest declines in mortality since 1990, primarily due to declines in infectious diseases. The leading cause of death among older children is injuries, including road traffic injuries and drowning.

Wondering who is the most vulnerable? - The answer is children under the age of five.



Children under the age of five are the most vulnerable to these preventable deaths. Although progress has been made globally to reduce the under-five mortality rate, there are significant disparities across regions and countries.

Sub-Saharan Africa has the highest mortality rate, with one child in 13 dying before their fifth birthday. Malnourished (unterernährt) children, particularly those with severe acute malnutrition, have a higher risk of death from common childhood illnesses. Nutrition-related factors contribute to about 45% of deaths in children under the age of 5.

To address the issue, we need:

  • Skilled attendants for antenatal, birth, and postnatal care
  • Access to nutrition and micronutrients
  • Family knowledge of danger signs in a child's health
  • Improved access to water, sanitation, and hygiene
  • Immunizations
These solutions can save lives and reduce mortality rates.

What can we do to help?


Even if you’re not a nurse nor a doctor you still can contribute to this global effort by donating money to certified organizations that invest in medicine or food for children in need. If you are unable to donate, you can still spread information to others who have the ability to donate.

Every little bit helps in this global effort to improve health and wellbeing.
